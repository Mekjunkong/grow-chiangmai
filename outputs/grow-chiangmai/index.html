Better idea

Make the farm a paid Chiang Mai experience first, not a monthly subscription first.

In Chiang Mai, the easier money is in beautiful day experiences and group visits. Monthly harvest beds can still exist — but they should be the follow-up product, not the first thing to prove.

Core offer

Sell Chiang Mai Farm Day Passes first. A paid 2–3 hour visit is easier to understand, easier to gift, and easier to buy than a monthly vegetable-bed subscription.

Phase 1

Run 3 paid farm days and one private group day. Use shade, water, hand-wash, borrowed/locked tools, photo spots, seedlings, and simple refreshments. Partner with a nearby cafe instead of building one.

Follow-up

Convert only the best visitors into Harvest Bed Club members. Members pay for a cared-for bed, weekly updates, visit days, and harvest options — not land ownership or guaranteed harvest.

Proof

Measure paid behavior, not compliments. Track ticket sales, no-shows, group inquiries, deposit conversion, weekly-update engagement, caretaker hours, and repeat bookings.

Pilot farm shape

A photogenic partner farm with a repeatable guest route.

Grow Chiang Mai operates as a Thai-run farm experience and harvest service. Guests buy a hosted visit, learning, photos, local connection, and optional harvest-bed follow-up. They do not buy land, own land, or receive a careless land promise.

  • Start with a guest route: arrival, shade, planting station, herb/tasting station, photo spot, hand-wash, and checkout.
  • Keep banana, herb, or cover-crop areas so the site feels like a real working farm, not a staged set.
  • Prepare only 10 harvest beds after paid visitors ask for a longer relationship.
  • Use weekly photos and field notes before any live-camera hardware.
  • Founding area can be Mae Rim, San Sai, Doi Saket, Hang Dong, Mae Taeng, or near Kakanok if access, water, title, and permissions are clean.

Trust note: real site photos matter. Paid promises should focus on the hosted experience, safe access, care, learning, and honest harvest expectations — not land ownership or guaranteed crops.

Later upgrades

Postpone AI, cameras, and cafe until the experience sells.

The first version should prove tickets, group bookings, reviews, and simple weekly updates manually. Only add sensors, live cameras, or AI irrigation after harvest members renew and pay for the update habit.

What stays manual in phase 1

Keep the first service simple and inspectable. A host runs the visit, a caretaker keeps the site safe and alive, and the operator records what guests ask to buy next.

  • Guest arrival checklist, safety note, and photo route.
  • Manual booking follow-up and review collection.
  • Short field note only for harvest members.
  • Simple post-visit question: would you book again, bring a group, or join a bed?
  • Upgrade wishlist tracked before any hardware spend.

Local loop

The local story becomes stronger when guests can see it.

Good intent is not enough. Farm days let people meet the land, the caretaker, the compost loop, and the real local work before they join anything monthly.

  • Paid work for village caretakers, hosts, workshop guides, and farm support.
  • Natural inputs sourced from local compost, rice husk, banana waste, leaf mold, or traceable minority and hilltribe suppliers when available.
  • Optional sponsor activities for schools, temples, or community food projects after the paid model works.
  • Clear field notes and photo rules so guests see crops without exposing people.

Clear before you join

Questions a serious member will ask.

The answers stay practical because the first business should prove paid visits before monthly memberships.

Are members renting Thai land?

No. The safer structure is a Thai-operated farm membership and service. Members receive access, care, learning, updates, and harvest options. Legal wording is kept clear before any paid membership is confirmed.

Is it certified organic?

Not yet. We use organic methods, natural inputs, compost, and careful growing practices. Certified organic is only claimed after real certification and recordkeeping.

Can I watch my plot on camera?

Not in the first version. The better business starts with farm-day visits and, later, weekly crop photos for Harvest Bed Club members. Live cameras are a paid upgrade only if members prove they value frequent updates.

Can AI water my harvest bed automatically?

Not at launch. Manual caretaker care comes first. AI irrigation only makes sense after the team understands water timing, crop failure, caretaker hours, and renewal behavior across real beds.

Why sell farm days before monthly beds?

Because Chiang Mai visitors already understand paid experiences. Farm days create trust, photos, reviews, and group referrals before the team takes on monthly crop-care risk.

What is the local fertilizer story?

The professional version is traceable, not vague. Buy quality compost or natural inputs from local producers, collect clean organic waste when it can be processed safely, and show field notes. Do not overclaim that this solves burning season.

Why keep banana rows on the land?

If the rented place already grows banana, keep it. It gives the farm a real productive backdrop while the paid beds prove demand. The public promise should be agricultural care, soil improvement, local inputs, and a good member experience.

What happens if my crop fails?

Farming has weather, insects, and timing risk. Managed members should receive field notes, replanting plans when possible, and honest seasonal expectations rather than guaranteed harvest claims.
