Professional pilot

The executable version starts with a partner plot, not a full farm buildout.

The idea becomes possible when the first promise is small: one accessible plot, one caretaker rhythm, one monthly crop cycle, and a paid trial day before any permanent cafe, camera, or AI investment.

Land

Start with partner or low-commitment rented land. The first site only needs safe access, water, shade, toilet access, and permission for 10 small beds plus weekend visits. Do not market a specific parcel until lease/use permission is clean.

Phase 1

Sell 2 paid trial days and 10 managed-bed deposits before building. Start with shade, water, hand-wash, borrowed/locked tools, clear signage, and simple refreshments. Use partner cafe or nearby cafe instead of building one first.

Promise

Service membership, not land ownership. Members pay for care, learning, updates, visit access, and harvest options. Keep legal wording simple and confirm payment only after the first site is usable.

Proof

Measure who actually pays. Track trial-day attendance, deposits, missed visits, caretaker hours, photo-update engagement, crop failure, and whether members renew after the first cycle.

Launch pricing logic

Pricing that proves demand before adding expensive features.

The first price is for learn-and-commit behavior: a trial day, then a small managed bed. Live cameras, cafe buildout, and AI are later upgrades, not launch requirements.

Farm learning benchmark

Local organic farm cooking classes commonly sit around THB 1,200 to THB 900 per person for a one-day experience.

AC comfort benchmark

Chiang Mai coworking day access often sits around THB 150 to THB 300, with monthly hot desks around THB 2,500 to THB 4,500.

Occasional live viewing can use meaningful mobile data, so camera access should be included in premium plans or priced as a paid add-on.

Pilot farm shape

A 2-rai site, with only the first beds sold.

Grow Chiang Mai operates as a Thai-run farm service. Members buy access, care, learning, updates, and community. They do not buy land, own land, or receive a careless land promise.

  • Start with 20 to 30 small plots, then expand toward 50 only after demand is proven.
  • Keep banana, herb, or cover-crop areas around beds so the land stays visibly productive.
  • Drip irrigation, water storage, compost/vermicompost area, cool room, and tool shed.
  • Live bed cameras with privacy signage before any member access.
  • Founding parcel preference: near Kakanok if access, water, title, and permissions are clean, otherwise Mae Rim, Mae Taeng, San Sai, Doi Saket, or Hang Dong.

Trust note: existing banana rows can stay as a practical farm backdrop while the membership beds prove demand. Paid member promises should focus on service, access, care, safety, and harvest expectations.

Later upgrades

Postpone AI and live cameras until the manual service works.

The first version should prove care, visits, photos, and renewals manually. Only add sensors, live cameras, or AI irrigation after 10+ paid beds show that members value updates enough to justify hardware and data costs.

What stays manual in phase 1

Keep the first service simple and inspectable. A caretaker checks beds, waters on schedule, takes weekly photos, and writes a short field note. This creates the data needed before automation.

  • Weekly crop photo from the same angle.
  • Short field note: watered, weeded, planted, harvested, issue found.
  • Manual crop-health check by caretaker/operator.
  • Simple renewal question after each crop cycle.
  • Upgrade wishlist tracked before any hardware spend.

Do not sell AI, cafe comfort, or live video as launch promises. Sell a small cared-for bed with honest updates first.

Local loop

The social impact has to be operational.

Good intent is not enough. The farm should show where the work, waste, soil, and money move.

  • Paid work for village caretakers, workshop guides, and farm support.
  • Natural inputs sourced from local compost, rice husk, banana waste, leaf mold, or traceable minority and hilltribe suppliers when available.
  • Optional sponsor beds for schools, temples, or community food projects.
  • Clear field notes and camera rules so members see crops without exposing people.
1

Collect clean local waste. Banana stems, leaves, cafe grounds, rice husk, and vegetable trimmings can become compost when sorted properly.

Reduce burn pressure carefully. Do not claim the farm stops burning season. Say it creates a small paid alternative for clean organic material.

Clear before you join

Questions a serious member will ask.

The answers stay practical because the first farm is still a pilot.

Are members renting Thai land?

No. The safer structure is a Thai-operated farm membership and service. Members receive access, care, learning, updates, and harvest options. Legal wording is kept clear before any paid membership is confirmed.

Is it certified organic?

Not yet. We use organic methods, natural inputs, compost, and careful growing practices. Certified organic is only claimed after real certification and recordkeeping.

Can I watch my plot on camera?

Not in the first version. The executable pilot starts with weekly crop photos and field notes. Live cameras are a later paid upgrade only if members prove they value frequent updates.

Can AI water my plot automatically?

Not at launch. Manual caretaker care comes first. AI irrigation only makes sense after the team understands water timing, crop failure, caretaker hours, and member renewal behavior across real beds.

Why make the 4-month plan cheaper?

Four months is long enough to plan a crop cycle and short enough for long-stay visitors. The discount also reduces churn, makes caretaker scheduling easier, and gives members a clear reason to stay through harvest.

What is the local fertilizer story?

The professional version is traceable, not vague. Buy quality compost or natural inputs from local producers, collect clean organic waste when it can be processed safely, and show field notes. Do not overclaim that this solves burning season.

Why keep banana rows on the land?

If the rented place already grows banana, keep it. It gives the farm a real productive backdrop while the paid beds prove demand. The public promise should be agricultural care, soil improvement, local inputs, and a good member experience.

What happens if my crop fails?

Farming has weather, insects, and timing risk. Managed members should receive field notes, replanting plans when possible, and honest seasonal expectations rather than guaranteed harvest claims.
